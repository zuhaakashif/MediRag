MEDICATION ERROR REPORTING PROTOCOL

Policy Reference: MED-OPS-004
Effective Date: January 2024
Review Date: January 2025
Applies To: All clinical and non-clinical staff

Definition:
A medication error is any preventable event that may cause or lead to inappropriate medication use or patient harm. This includes prescribing errors, dispensing errors, administration errors, monitoring failures, and near-misses.

Immediate Steps (First Responder):
1. Ensure patient safety — assess the patient immediately; call for clinical support if required
2. Do not leave the patient unattended if harm may have occurred
3. Notify the senior nurse or doctor on duty within 15 minutes of discovery
4. Document the incident in the patient's medical record factually and without speculation
5. Do not alter, delete, or add retrospective entries to the patient record

Reporting Procedure:
1. Complete an incident report in the hospital's risk management system (Datix) within 24 hours
2. Include: date/time, medication name, intended vs. actual dose, route, patient identifiers, witnesses
3. Submit to your line manager and the ward pharmacist on the same day
4. For serious incidents (patient hospitalisation, significant harm, or death): escalate immediately to the Clinical Governance Team and the Duty Manager

Escalation Contacts:
- Duty Manager (24/7): Ext. 4400
- Clinical Governance Team: Ext. 3210 (Mon–Fri, 09:00–17:00)
- Out-of-hours Clinical Governance: Bleep 774
- Hospital Pharmacist (on-call): Ext. 2100

Confidentiality:
Incident reports are used for quality improvement and are protected under clinical governance frameworks. Staff should report in good faith without fear of blame. Deliberate concealment of medication errors is a disciplinary matter.

PATIENT DISCHARGE CHECKLIST — STANDARD PROCEDURE

Policy Reference: DISC-OPS-007
Applies To: Ward nurses, junior doctors, discharge coordinators

Pre-Discharge Steps:
1. Confirm discharge decision with the responsible consultant or registrar
2. Complete the TTO (To Take Out) medication list — checked by ward pharmacist
3. Ensure patient has received discharge medication counselling (pharmacist or nurse)
4. Print and issue discharge summary to patient — one copy to GP electronically via system
5. Book any required follow-up outpatient appointments before patient leaves
6. Arrange transport if required (social work referral if needed — Ext. 5500)
7. Complete nursing discharge note in the EPR system
8. Remove IV cannulas, catheters, or monitoring devices as appropriate
9. Patient to sign discharge consent form (Form DIS-01)
10. Confirm patient has a safe place to go and a responsible adult if needed

Documentation Requirements:
- Discharge summary must be completed within 24 hours of discharge (mandatory KPI)
- Late discharge summaries must be flagged to the ward manager
- Any outstanding investigations must be listed with clear follow-up plan

If Patient Refuses Discharge:
- Document refusal in EPR with date, time, and reason given by patient
- Escalate to the responsible consultant within 2 hours
- If safeguarding concern: contact Social Work Team immediately (Ext. 5500)

ON-CALL ESCALATION PATHWAY — NIGHTS AND WEEKENDS

Policy Reference: ONCALL-OPS-002
Applies To: All on-call staff, ward nurses, reception

General Principle:
All clinical concerns outside normal hours should follow the escalation ladder below. Do not skip levels unless the situation is immediately life-threatening (proceed directly to Step 4).

Step 1 — Ward Level:
Contact the on-call junior doctor (SHO/FY1) for the ward. Bleep numbers are posted at each nurses' station and updated weekly.

Step 2 — Registrar:
If the SHO is unavailable or the concern is beyond their competency, escalate to the on-call registrar for the relevant specialty. Bleep board at main reception.

Step 3 — Consultant On-Call:
Contact via switchboard (Ext. 0) for specialty consultant on-call. Required for: ICU admissions, major haemorrhage protocol activation, unexpected deaths, critical test results.

Step 4 — Emergency Response:
- Cardiac Arrest / Medical Emergency: Dial 2222 (internal) — state location and nature of emergency
- Fire: Dial 2222 — state "fire" and location
- Security Threat: Dial 2222 — state "security" and location

SBAR Communication Tool:
When escalating any clinical concern, use SBAR format:
- S (Situation): What is happening right now?
- B (Background): Relevant medical history and reason for admission
- A (Assessment): What do you think the problem is?
- R (Recommendation): What do you need from the person you are calling?

SHIFT HANDOVER PROTOCOL

Policy Reference: HANDOVER-OPS-001
Applies To: All nursing and medical staff

Handover must occur at every shift change and must not be rushed. Inadequate handover is a leading cause of preventable patient harm.

Nursing Handover (Bedside):
- Conduct at the bedside for every patient — patient can be included where appropriate
- Use the hospital's standardised handover form (SBAR-based, available on EPR)
- Cover: patient name, diagnosis, overnight events, observations trend, outstanding jobs, family concerns, discharge plan
- Flag: deteriorating patients, patients for review, those with escalating NEWS2 scores

Medical Handover (Structured):
- Evening handover: 17:00 — outgoing team presents sick and at-risk patients to on-call team
- Morning handover: 08:00 — on-call team presents overnight events to day team
- Location: Medical Education Room, Level 2 (or virtual via Teams if applicable)
- Attendance mandatory for all doctors finishing or starting a shift

Sick Patient Flagging:
Any patient with NEWS2 ≥5 must be handed over verbally and documented as a priority. The on-call doctor must acknowledge receipt of this information.

CONSENT PROCEDURE — ADULTS WITH CAPACITY

Policy Reference: CONSENT-OPS-003
Legal Basis: Mental Capacity Act 2005

Principles:
- Every adult patient is assumed to have capacity unless assessed otherwise
- Consent must be voluntary, informed, and given by a person with capacity
- Implied consent is acceptable for routine non-invasive procedures (e.g., taking a pulse)
- Written consent is required for surgical procedures, sedation, general anaesthesia, and participation in research

Process for Written Consent:
1. The clinician performing the procedure must obtain consent (cannot be delegated to someone unable to perform the procedure)
2. Provide written patient information leaflet relevant to the procedure at least 24 hours in advance where possible
3. Discuss benefits, risks (including common and serious), alternatives, and consequences of declining
4. Allow the patient time to ask questions
5. Complete Consent Form 1 (adults with capacity) — available on EPR and at ward admin desks
6. File signed consent form in the patient's medical record before procedure begins

If Patient Lacks Capacity:
- Conduct formal Mental Capacity Assessment (MCA) and document
- Apply best interests principle — consult family/carers and MDT
- Use Consent Form 4 (treatment in best interests)
- Contact Legal Services if there is dispute: Ext. 6600
