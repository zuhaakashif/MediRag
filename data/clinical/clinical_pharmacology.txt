IBUPROFEN — CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY REFERENCE

Drug Class: Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drug (NSAID), Propionic acid derivative
Mechanism of Action: Non-selective inhibition of cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) and cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), reducing synthesis of prostaglandins and thromboxanes. This results in analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory effects.

Indications:
- Mild to moderate pain (acute musculoskeletal, post-operative, dysmenorrhea)
- Inflammatory conditions: osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis
- Fever management in adults and pediatric patients
- Migraine with or without aura (adjunct)

Dosing — Adults:
- Analgesic/Antipyretic: 200–400 mg PO q4–6h PRN; max 1200 mg/day (OTC), 3200 mg/day (Rx)
- Anti-inflammatory: 400–800 mg PO TID–QID; max 3200 mg/day
- Dysmenorrhea: 400 mg PO q4h PRN

Dosing — Pediatric:
- Fever/Pain: 5–10 mg/kg PO q6–8h; max 40 mg/kg/day
- JIA: 30–40 mg/kg/day divided q6–8h

Contraindications:
- Known hypersensitivity to ibuprofen or other NSAIDs
- Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD) / aspirin triad
- Active peptic ulcer disease or GI bleeding
- Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min)
- Perioperative pain in CABG surgery
- Third trimester of pregnancy (premature closure of ductus arteriosus)

Drug Interactions — Clinically Significant:
- Warfarin: Increased bleeding risk (COX-1 inhibition impairs platelet function + potential GI bleed)
- ACE Inhibitors / ARBs: Reduced antihypertensive efficacy; risk of acute kidney injury (triple whammy)
- Lithium: NSAIDs reduce renal lithium clearance; monitor serum lithium levels
- Methotrexate: NSAIDs reduce renal clearance of MTX; risk of MTX toxicity
- SSRIs: Additive bleeding risk
- Corticosteroids: Additive GI toxicity risk
- Diuretics: Reduced diuretic efficacy; risk of renal impairment

Adverse Effects:
- GI: Dyspepsia, nausea, peptic ulcer, GI haemorrhage (most common serious ADR)
- Renal: Acute kidney injury, sodium and water retention, hypertension, interstitial nephritis
- CV: Increased risk of MI and stroke with prolonged use (dose-dependent); avoid in established CVD
- Haematological: Reversible platelet inhibition, anaemia
- Hepatic: Transaminase elevation (rare); hepatotoxicity (very rare)
- CNS: Headache, dizziness, tinnitus (especially at high doses)
- Hypersensitivity: Urticaria, bronchospasm, anaphylaxis (especially in AERD patients)

Monitoring Parameters:
- Renal function (BUN, creatinine) in at-risk patients
- Blood pressure in hypertensive patients
- CBC in chronic users
- Signs of GI bleeding (haemoccult, Hb)
- Hepatic function in chronic use

Special Populations:
- Elderly: Use lowest effective dose; increased risk of GI and renal ADRs; consider PPI co-prescription
- Renal impairment: Avoid if CrCl <30; use with caution if CrCl 30–60
- Hepatic impairment: Use with caution in mild-moderate; avoid in severe
- Pregnancy: Avoid in 3rd trimester; use with caution in 1st/2nd trimester
- Breastfeeding: Compatible with breastfeeding at standard doses (low milk transfer)

ICD-10 Codes for Common Indications:
- M79.3 — Panniculitis (pain management)
- M06.9 — Rheumatoid arthritis, unspecified
- N94.4 — Primary dysmenorrhoea
- R50.9 — Fever, unspecified
- M19.90 — Osteoarthritis, unspecified

PARACETAMOL (ACETAMINOPHEN) — CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY REFERENCE

Drug Class: Para-aminophenol derivative; analgesic and antipyretic (not classified as NSAID)
Mechanism of Action: Exact mechanism incompletely understood. Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis centrally (CNS); possible modulation of endocannabinoid system and serotonergic descending pain pathways. Minimal peripheral anti-inflammatory effect.

Indications:
- Mild to moderate pain
- Fever
- First-line analgesic in osteoarthritis (per NICE/ACR guidelines)
- Safe analgesic in pregnancy and breastfeeding

Dosing — Adults:
- 500 mg–1000 mg PO/IV q4–6h; max 4000 mg/day (healthy adults), 2000 mg/day (hepatic risk)
- IV: 1000 mg over 15 minutes q6h (max 4g/day)

Hepatotoxicity — Critical Points:
- Hepatotoxicity occurs via accumulation of NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) when glucuronidation and sulfation pathways are saturated
- Risk factors: Chronic alcohol use, fasting/malnutrition, hepatic disease, CYP2E1 inducers (rifampicin, INH)
- Overdose treatment: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — most effective within 8–10 hours of ingestion
- Rumack-Matthew nomogram guides NAC therapy in overdose

Drug Interactions:
- Warfarin: Chronic use (>2g/day) may potentiate anticoagulation — INR monitoring advised
- Alcohol: Increased hepatotoxicity risk
- Rifampicin/INH: CYP2E1 induction increases NAPQI formation

HYPERTENSION — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL

Definition (2023 ESC/ESH Guidelines):
- Grade 1: SBP 140–159 / DBP 90–99 mmHg
- Grade 2: SBP 160–179 / DBP 100–109 mmHg
- Grade 3: SBP ≥180 / DBP ≥110 mmHg
- Isolated Systolic Hypertension: SBP ≥140, DBP <90

Diagnosis:
- Confirm with ≥2 readings on ≥2 separate occasions
- ABPM or HBPM preferred to exclude white-coat hypertension
- Target organ damage assessment: ECG, urine ACR, eGFR, fundoscopy

First-Line Antihypertensive Agents:
1. ACE Inhibitors (e.g., Ramipril 2.5–10 mg OD) — preferred in DM, CKD, post-MI, HFrEF
2. ARBs (e.g., Losartan 25–100 mg OD) — preferred if ACE inhibitor intolerant (cough)
3. CCBs (e.g., Amlodipine 5–10 mg OD) — preferred in elderly, isolated systolic HTN, Afro-Caribbean patients
4. Thiazide diuretics (e.g., Indapamide 1.5 mg OD) — preferred in elderly, heart failure

Combination Therapy (AB/CD Rule):
- Step 2: ACE-I/ARB + CCB or Thiazide
- Step 3: ACE-I/ARB + CCB + Thiazide
- Step 4 (Resistant HTN): Add spironolactone 25–50 mg OD or alpha/beta blocker

Hypertensive Emergency:
- SBP >180 with end-organ damage (AKI, encephalopathy, ALHF, aortic dissection)
- IV labetalol or sodium nitroprusside; reduce MAP by no more than 25% in first hour
- Admit to HDU/ICU

DIABETES MELLITUS TYPE 2 — MANAGEMENT SUMMARY

Diagnostic Criteria (WHO):
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥7.0 mmol/L
- 2-hr plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L (OGTT)
- HbA1c ≥48 mmol/mol (6.5%)
- Random plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L + symptoms

Glycaemic Targets:
- HbA1c: <53 mmol/mol (7.0%) general; <48 mmol/mol (6.5%) if on lifestyle/metformin only
- Individualise targets in elderly, frail, or hypoglycaemia-prone patients

First-Line: Metformin
- Start 500 mg OD/BD with food; titrate to 1000 mg BD over 4 weeks
- Contraindicated: eGFR <30; withhold if eGFR <45 for contrast procedures
- ADRs: GI (mitigated by slow titration); lactic acidosis (rare)

Second-Line Options:
- SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin): preferred in established CVD, HF, CKD
- GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide): preferred in obesity, CVD risk reduction
- DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin): weight-neutral, low hypoglycaemia risk
- Sulfonylureas (gliclazide): cheap, effective, risk of hypoglycaemia and weight gain
- Insulin: Basal insulin (glargine/detemir) when oral agents insufficient

Monitoring:
- HbA1c every 3–6 months until stable, then every 6 months
- Annual: eGFR, urine ACR, lipids, BP, foot exam, retinal screening, BMI

ISOTRETINOIN (ACCUTANE) — CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY REFERENCE

Drug Class: Retinoid (Vitamin A derivative)
Mechanism of Action: Reduces sebaceous gland size and sebum production; inhibits P. acnes proliferation; normalises keratinocyte differentiation and reduces follicular hyperkeratinisation.

Indications:
- Severe nodular/cystic acne refractory to conventional therapy including antibiotics
- Acne with significant scarring risk
- Acne causing psychological distress

Dosing — Adults and Adolescents (≥12 years):
- Starting dose: 0.5 mg/kg/day PO in 1–2 divided doses with food
- Maintenance: 0.5–1.0 mg/kg/day based on response and tolerability
- Target cumulative dose: 120–150 mg/kg total (to minimise relapse)
- Duration: typically 16–24 weeks per course
- Adolescent females (e.g., 15-year-old, ~50 kg): typically start 25 mg/day (0.5 mg/kg); may increase to 50 mg/day
- Maximum single dose: 1 mg/kg/day; max 80 mg/day in most patients

Contraindications:
- Pregnancy (Category X — highly teratogenic; causes severe fetal malformations)
- Breastfeeding
- Hypersensitivity to isotretinoin or parabens (in capsule formulation)
- Concurrent tetracycline antibiotics (risk of pseudotumour cerebri)
- Severe hepatic impairment

Mandatory Precautions — Female Patients of Childbearing Age:
- iPLEDGE programme (USA) or equivalent pregnancy prevention programme required
- Must use two effective forms of contraception for 1 month before, during, and 1 month after treatment
- Pregnancy test required before each monthly prescription
- Monthly follow-up mandatory

Monitoring:
- Baseline: LFTs, fasting lipids, FBC, pregnancy test (females)
- Monthly: pregnancy test (females), LFTs, fasting lipids
- Discontinue if: ALT/AST >3x ULN, triglycerides >800 mg/dL, signs of pseudotumour cerebri

Adverse Effects:
- Very common: Cheilitis (near universal), dry skin/mucosae, epistaxis, myalgia
- Common: Elevated triglycerides and LFTs, photosensitivity, arthralgias
- Serious: Teratogenicity, pseudotumour cerebri (esp. with tetracyclines), severe depression/suicidal ideation (monitor closely), inflammatory bowel disease (rare)
- Rare: Stevens-Johnson syndrome, hearing impairment, corneal opacities

Drug Interactions:
- Tetracyclines: Contraindicated — additive risk of pseudotumour cerebri (raised intracranial pressure)
- Vitamin A supplements: Additive toxicity risk — avoid concurrent use
- Methotrexate: Additive hepatotoxicity — avoid
- Carbamazepine: Reduced isotretinoin levels

Paediatric Considerations:
- Approved for ≥12 years
- Dosing based on weight (mg/kg)
- Growth plate concerns in young adolescents — use lowest effective dose
- Psychiatric monitoring essential in adolescents; inform parents of mood change risk
- For a 15-year-old female: standard starting dose 0.5 mg/kg/day; weight-based calculation required; mandatory pregnancy prevention programme
